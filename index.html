Why Round Rock homes spring leaks

Three things gang up on the pipes here: hard water, shifting ground, and the age of the plumbing.

Round Rock sits on some of the harder water in Central Texas. Since June 2020 the city's main supply has flowed from Lake Travis through the BCRUA partnership, testing near 15.2 grains per gallon. That heavy mineral load builds scale inside copper supply lines and gnaws at the metal from within. Give it enough years and you get the pinhole failures that surface behind drywall and beneath slabs, usually in the aging copper around town.

The ground fights the pipes too, and this town is unusual because it straddles two soil zones. The west half rests on Hill Country limestone, a porous karst that lets a leak travel sideways and emerge far from where it began. The east half leans into Blackland Prairie clay, which swells after rain and shrinks hard during drought. That constant motion tugs on slab plumbing and sewer laterals until a joint separates. Which half of town your house sits on changes how we hunt the source, so we ask up front.

Age is the third strike. Houses in Forest Creek, Brushy Creek, and Cat Hollow rose in the late 1980s and 1990s, right after Dell planted its headquarters here. Their original copper now runs thirty to forty years old, squarely inside the window where pinholes begin. Newer builds out in Teravista, Paloma Lake, and Mayfield Ranch rely on PEX, which shrugs off mineral buildup better yet still fails at fittings and joints. Old Town holds the oldest stock of all, where galvanized supply and cast iron drains rust and split as the decades pass.

Pre-1955 — Old Town

Galvanized supply and cast iron drains. Both corrode from the inside out.

1965–1985 — Forest Creek Era

Copper supply lines now 40+ years old and entering the pinhole window.

1990s–Now — Teravista & Paloma

PEX resists scale buildup but fails at fittings and manifold connections.

Weather sets the clock. Central Texas swings between deep drought and sudden cloudbursts, and the city falls inside what locals call Flash Flood Alley, where limestone funnels storm runoff straight into Brushy Creek. When a long dry spell breaks, the eastern clay rehydrates and heaves, and that jolt is often what finishes a slab line already under strain. Service calls cluster after the first heavy rain following a drought, and the dry spring of 2026 keeps that pattern alive.

Non-Invasive Leak Detection in Round Rock, TX

A hidden leak does not have to mean a wall you tear out. We begin with detection. Acoustic sensors listen for the hiss of pressurized water escaping a pipe. Thermal cameras read the faint temperature shift a leak leaves on a floor or wall. Electronic tracing and pressure testing map the run and narrow the spot. By the time we cut, we already know where.

On the limestone west side that patience pays off, since water there can emerge yards from the actual break. Chasing the wet patch alone would mean opening the wrong place. We trace the pipe instead. When the repair calls for it, a spot fix, a reroute, or a trenchless pull can solve the trouble without a full excavation. If you would rather talk it through first, call (512) 737-6168 and describe what you notice.

Different faults call for different tools, and we bring the ones that fit the job. Acoustic gear works well on pressurized supply lines under a slab. Thermal imaging shines on hot-water seeps and drips behind drywall. A camera runs the sewer line when the trouble is a drain or a lateral. Tracer gas and line tracing handle the breaks that hide from sound, like a slow weep through dry, rocky ground. Matching the method to the fault is most of the work, and it explains why a careful locate beats guessing every time.

Signs you may have a hidden leak

Any one of these is worth a phone call. Catching a leak early is the difference between a small repair and a torn-up floor.

  • Your water bill jumped and your habits did not.
  • A spot on the slab feels warm under bare feet.
  • A patch of yard stays green and soggy through dry weather.
  • The meter keeps creeping with every tap shut off.
  • Water pressure dropped without an obvious reason.
  • You hear water moving inside a wall when nothing is running.

Will insurance cover it?

Sometimes, and sometimes not. Most Texas policies pay for the sudden damage a leak causes but not the tired pipe behind it, and adjusters deny claims on technicalities more often than people expect. A claim can hinge on whether the harm was abrupt or gradual, whether you reported it quickly, and how clearly the cause is documented. We photograph our findings and log our readings, so you hand the adjuster a clean record, which often decides a paid claim versus a drawn-out fight.

Our blog explains how local claims win approval and where they collapse, in plain language. If you are staring at a wet ceiling and a policy you have never opened, ring (512) 737-6168 first. Halting the water and recording the cause are the two moves that help most, and both start with a look.

24/7 Emergency Leak Service in Round Rock, TX

Burst pipe, active flood, water rushing behind a wall — some leaks cannot wait until morning.

Burst pipe emergency

If a pipe burst, find your main shutoff valve first. It is usually near the water meter or in a utility closet. Turn it clockwise to stop the flow, then call. We dispatch a licensed Round Rock crew any hour of the day.

Active flooding or water intrusion

Shut off electricity to the affected area if water is near outlets or panels. Do not enter standing water if there is any risk of electrical contact. Move valuables out of the water line if it is safe to do so, then call us immediately.

Gas leak or mixed emergency

A plumbing emergency with a gas smell is a different situation. Leave the building, do not use switches or phones inside, and call 911 or Atmos Energy before calling us. Gas leaks require the utility company on scene first.
